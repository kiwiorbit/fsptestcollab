Why are interactions at home the best?

In the familial environment, where parents hold valuable insights into their child's needs, the primary focus remains steadfastly on nurturing their well-being and development. Emphasizing the creation of a calm and supportive atmosphere, disruptions are minimized to enhance learning opportunities. This is particularly crucial during the first 1,000 days of life, a key learning window where every experience profoundly shapes a child's future path.

‚ú®Building brain through Conversations,

Did you know that a playful exchange of words could build brains? With LENA technology, we measure these precious "conversational turns," counting each time an adult speaks and a child chimes in (or vice versa) within five seconds. From a baby's giggle to a toddler's pretend words, every sound matters. These turns are LENA‚Äôs way of capturing the vital "serve and return" interactions that spark brain development!

LENA Device

The LENA device, a compact and child-friendly recorder, is designed for children to wear for a day. It adheres to both U.S. and international safety regulations for electronics and toys. Unlike a cellphone, it doesn't transmit data and employs low-power processors similar to those found in hearing aids.

LENA Software

The LENA software analyzes the audio recorded by the device, converting it into valuable information about speech. This data is then presented in easily understandable reports that can be shared with caregivers. Through secure online tools, program management becomes efficient, with real-time assessments of fidelity and effectiveness built-in.

‚ú®Call for participation in Caregiver's Coaching Study

Published on May 8, 2023

We require families of children with hearing loss to take part in the study. Caregivers and their children with hearing loss will participate in pre-post assessments and 3 training sessions either in person or via video link. We will support parents on how to interact and engage their children with hearing loss in different natural play-based learning activities. We will only require full-day LENA recordings to collect data. and data is automatically deleted once its has its derived useful information.

Who can participate?

The research study is open to children with hearing loss and their parents or caregivers. Caregivers can include parents or any other adults responsible for the child's care. Families are eligible to participate if they have a child with hearing loss who meets the following criteria:

  • ‚úîÔ∏è Passed Newborn Hearing Screening.
  • ‚úîÔ∏è Diagnosed with hearing loss between 3-6 months of age.
  • ‚úîÔ∏è Regularly uses hearing devices such as cochlear implants or hearing aids.
  • ‚úîÔ∏è Resides in a home where one or more languages are spoken.
  • ‚úîÔ∏è Primarily speaks English, particularly within the household.
  • ‚úîÔ∏è Can copy some spoken words.
  • ‚úîÔ∏è Does not have any other disabilities aside from hearing loss.

Compensation/Gift

Participants will receive a $25 E-voucher for each training session attended, as recognition of their time and the associated costs of participating in the research. Additionally, families will be provided with a $10 voucher for mobile phone data top-ups to facilitate their participation in phone call follow-ups. Interested families are encouraged to contact the research team for more information on participation.

Meet Our Team

We are coaching parents/caregivers to expose optimal quantity and quality of interaction with their children with hearing differences.

Nuzhat Sultana

Lecturer/SLT

Dr. Nuzhat Sultana completed her Ph.D from The University of Hongkong, She is a Lecturer of Speech Science in the School of Psychology at The University of Auckland, Currently her research is focused on language development in young children, focusing on those with and without hearing loss. Her work includes early intervention and speech-language therapy to support caregiver-child interactions. She is developing a programme to help parents optimize verbal interactions using LENA technology. This research benefits children with hearing loss and other SLC challenges. Dr. Sultana also supervises research and provides professional guidance for speech-language therapists and students.

Suzanne Purdy

Professor

Suzanne (Te Rarawa, NgƒÅi Takoto) completed her MSc at University of Auckland, then trained as a clinical audiologist at the University of Melbourne in 1981. She worked as a clinical audiologist for six years but was lured back into hearing-related research. She then completed a PhD at the University of Iowa before starting her academic career in 1990. Her many hearing-related interests include sensory neuroscience, auditory processing, clinical audiology and equity in hearing health services. Due to the close link with audiology, she is very curious about auditory imagery as part of the rich tapestry of human auditory experiences.

Aleah Brock

Assistant Professor

Aleah is an assistant professor at the University of West Georgia, USA. She completed her PhD at the University of Georgia with research focused on coaching caregivers to use facilitative language techniques with their children who are deaf or hard of hearing (DHH). Prior to obtaining her PhD, Aleah worked as a speech-language pathologist at the Georgia School for the Deaf and continues to provide early intervention services to families of children who are DHH. Her current research interests investigate the impact of family-centered early intervention on language outcomes for children who are DHH, with a particular focus on optimizing caregivers' linguistic input to their children.

Liz Fairgray

Clinical Educator

Liz Fairgray is a Speech-Language Therapist and Auditory Verbal Therapist with 30 years of expertise in helping children with communication difficulties, particularly those with hearing loss. She received her postgraduate degree in Speech Pathology & Audiology in California and was the first New Zealander to become a Certified Auditory Verbal Therapist. Liz played a crucial role as the founding therapist at The Hearing House, emphasizing parent support as a cornerstone of her approach. She continues to advance her field through research, teaching, and serving as the NZSTA Expert Advisor for Child Hearing Loss. Currently, Liz is focused on supporting parents of children with hearing loss, building on her lifelong commitment to this area.
